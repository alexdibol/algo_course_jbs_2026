"A strategy does not deserve trust because it belongs to a familiar category. It deserves trust only if the mechanism underlying it can be made structurally legible and if that mechanism survives perturbation." — From the Course Introduction

Most algorithmic trading education fails in one of three ways. It is too theoretical to become executable. Too technical to become conceptually meaningful. Or too promotional to be intellectually trustworthy.

This course was built to resolve that fragmentation. It treats a trading system not as a collection of signals and indicators, but as a complete engineering discipline — from data governance through strategy design to market mechanics, implementation discipline, and systemic stress.

Three pillars form the intellectual core. A new methodological bridge shows how to move from research to implementation using agents without surrendering governance, human accountability, or institutional defensibility.

§ 01

What Makes This Different

First Principle
Mechanism Before Prediction

Most courses obsess over signal quality while treating execution, costs, and regime change as afterthoughts. This course inverts that hierarchy. Understanding why a strategy works — and under what structural conditions it fails — is the prerequisite for trusting it at all.

Second Principle
Survival Before Optimization

A strategy that cannot survive a liquidity squeeze, a correlation compression, or a funding crisis is not a strategy — it is a bet wearing a costume. Every chapter in this course asks: what is this system implicitly short, and what happens when that position is called in?

Third Principle
Governance Inside the Code

Causal integrity, reproducible experiments, auditable artifacts, and explicit assumptions are not bureaucratic add-ons. They are part of model validity. A system that cannot be reproduced, audited, and stress-tested is epistemically fragile — regardless of its backtest metrics.

§ 02

The Bridge from Research to Implementation

In addition to the three core pillars, Algo Systems now includes a dedicated cross-pillar methodological bridge: a book and a set of accompanying Colab notebooks that show how modern AI can be used to move from research artifacts to implementation-ready systems through specialized agents. This bridge does not replace the pillars. It connects them. Its purpose is to show, in a governed way, how a research idea becomes code, how code becomes an interpretable object, how that object is evaluated under fail-closed logic, and how research environments can be translated into deployment-oriented workflows without sacrificing transparency, accountability, or defensibility.

Cross-Pillar Methodological Layer
Agentic Research-to-Implementation Architecture

The bridge is designed for a modern reality: research often begins in notebooks, ideas often begin in prose, and implementation often lives in stricter environments such as QuantConnect and related production-facing systems. The challenge is not merely technical translation. The challenge is preserving meaning, making assumptions visible, and keeping the workflow institutionally reviewable.

The book and notebooks in this layer introduce a governed architecture of agents that help transform fragmented strategy work into a coherent lifecycle: generation, interpretation, evaluation, migration, and reporting. The emphasis is not automation theater. The emphasis is disciplined transformation under evidence, auditability, and human control.
